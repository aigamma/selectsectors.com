RSI mean reversion.

Compute Wilder's RSI over a period-bar window. Enter long when RSI dips below oversold; exit to flat when RSI rises above overbought. A fade-the-dip strategy that exploits short-term oversold bounces in trending markets.

Intuition

RSI is a 0-100 oscillator that measures the recent balance of up-day vs down-day moves. RSI near 100 means almost all recent bars closed higher than the prior; RSI near 0 means almost all closed lower. The conventional reading is that RSI below 30 is "oversold" (the asset has fallen sharply and is due for a bounce) and RSI above 70 is "overbought" (it has risen sharply and is due for a pause).

The mean-reversion strategy takes the oversold reading as a long entry signal and waits for RSI to climb back above overbought to exit. The implicit bet is that short-term extreme moves are followed by reversion to a more typical range.

When it works

In range-bound markets and on assets that have a strong underlying support level (or a Fed-style policy floor). The short-term oversold reading tends to mean-revert when the asset is not in a structural downtrend; the strategy captures the bounce and exits on the next overbought.

When it fails

In sustained downtrends. RSI can stay below 30 for weeks or months while the asset continues to fall. The strategy enters at the first oversold reading and rides the trend all the way down, exiting only when RSI eventually rises above overbought (which may require a substantial bounce that doesn't recover the initial loss). The 2008 financial crisis was an extended period of "oversold gets more oversold" that punished mean-reversion strategies broadly.

Parameters

  • period: RSI window length, in bars. Wilder originally suggested 14. Range: 2-100.
  • oversold: enter-long threshold. Typical: 20-30. Range: 0-100. Must be strictly less than overbought.
  • overbought: exit threshold. Typical: 65-80. Range: 0-100.

The Rust

The Wilder smoothing recurrence is the interesting part. Notice the seeding step that uses a simple mean of the first period gain/loss observations and the weight ratio (period - 1) / period on the prior average.
