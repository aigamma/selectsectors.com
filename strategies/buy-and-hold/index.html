Buy and hold.

Always long, no parameters. The strategy is the reference benchmark every other strategy is judged against; if a fancier strategy doesn't beat buy and hold on the same universe and date range, the complexity is not earning its keep.

Intuition

Buy on the first bar, hold to the last, no exits. The position vector is all 1.0 across the entire bar series. Realized return at bar i is simply the bar return times 1 (full exposure), which means the strategy's cumulative return equals the cumulative price return of the asset itself.

When it works

On any asset that drifts up over the test window. Equity indices over multi-year horizons tend to favor buy and hold because the long-run equity premium is positive; you get paid to take risk and stay in. The strategy's max drawdown, however, equals the asset's max drawdown, which can be deeply painful (SPX fell 50%+ in 2008-2009 and 35%+ in March 2020).

When it fails

On assets that decline over the test window. If the universe is selected with hindsight (post-bankruptcy survivors only), buy and hold looks great; if the universe is selected without hindsight (you would have held GE through 2000-2020), it looks much worse. This is the survivorship-bias trap and is why the universe selection is as important as the strategy.

Parameters

None. The strategy has nothing to tune.

The Rust

The shortest strategy in the crate. Just an empty-input guard and a vec of 1.0s the same length as the input bars.

crates/backtest-core/src/strategies/buy_and_hold.rs
