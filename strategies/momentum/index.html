Time-series momentum.

Long when today's close exceeds the close lookback bars ago; flat otherwise. Equivalent to the classic 12-1 momentum signal at smaller scales. Cheap to compute and surprisingly competitive at horizons in the 60-250 bar range on equity index series.

Intuition

"Has this asset gone up over the last N days? If yes, go long. If no, stay flat." That's the entire decision rule. The signal asks one question: is the most recent close higher than a reference close from some time back? The choice of how far back is the only knob.

When it works

Famously well across many asset classes and decades. The original Jegadeesh-Titman paper (1993) documented strong momentum returns in US equities at 3-12 month horizons; subsequent work extended it to commodities, currencies, and indices. The reason it works is debated (behavioral underreaction, slow information diffusion, risk-premium compensation) but the empirical pattern is robust enough that it has its own factor model.

When it fails

At very short horizons (lookback < 20 bars) the signal becomes noise-dominated and reverses; mean-reversion dominates at short horizons in many equity series. At very long horizons (lookback > 252 bars / 1 year) the signal becomes too sluggish to react to genuine reversals. The sweet spot is the 60-250-bar range that the academic literature converges on.

Momentum also crashes spectacularly during sharp regime changes. The 2009 momentum crash (when previously losing stocks ripped higher in the post-Lehman bounce) cost a long-only momentum strategy ~20% in a single quarter. History suggests these crashes are infrequent but severe.

Parameters

  • lookback: number of bars to compare against. Typical values: 20-250. Defaults to 60. Must be at least 1; the bar series must have at least lookback + 1 bars.
