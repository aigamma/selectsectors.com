Donchian breakout.

Long when today's close meets or exceeds the rolling high of the prior lookback bars; flat otherwise. The simplest version of the "trade the new high" idea Richard Donchian popularized in the 1960s and that Richard Dennis later codified for the Turtle Traders in the 1980s.

Intuition

A new N-bar high is a statement: the asset just broke through a level that has held for the entire prior lookback window. That break is interpreted as fresh demand and a continuation signal. We go long on the breakout and stay long until the close falls back below the rolling high (which means the breakout failed or the trend has paused).

The Turtles' full system layered position-sizing, volatility-based stops, and a symmetric short on N-bar lows. This site's version is just the long-side entry; the symmetric short would need risk-of-ruin work that isn't baked into the WASM crate yet.

When it works

On assets with persistent trends that break through prior highs and run further. Commodity bull markets are the classic case (the Turtles made their fortunes on commodity breakouts in the early 1980s); equity index breakouts through prior tops have also paid off historically when the breakout was confirmed by volume and broader market breadth.

When it fails

On false breakouts. A close at a new high that immediately reverses generates an entry that loses on the very next bar. Choppy markets where the rolling high is constantly tested and broken by tiny amounts produce a steady stream of these whipsaws. The longer the lookback, the rarer the breakout and the more meaningful when it happens, but the slower the strategy is to react to genuine trend reversals.

Parameters

  • lookback: number of prior bars to compute the rolling high over. Typical values: 20-100. Defaults to 20. Must be at least 2 (a 1-bar lookback degenerates to single-bar momentum).

The implementation uses the high field of each bar rather than the close; the breakout idea is "today's close exceeds the historical ceiling", and the ceiling is the prior high not the prior close. For SPX (where the WASM input projects close into all OHLC fields because the upstream table has no high) this collapses to a close-vs-prior-close comparison, which loses some fidelity.
