Bollinger Bands mean reversion.

Compute a rolling SMA and rolling standard deviation over a period-bar window. Construct the lower band at SMA − k × std. Enter long when the close drops below the lower band; exit to flat when the close rises back above the SMA. A dispersion-based fade-the- dip strategy, complementary to RSI mean reversion.

Intuition

Bollinger Bands assume that prices revert toward a rolling mean and that excursions beyond a fixed multiple of the rolling standard deviation are uncommon enough to bet on. When the close drops below the lower band, the price is unusually far from its short-term centerline; the bet is that it bounces back. The exit at the SMA centerline is the more pragmatic of the two exit choices (centerline vs upper band); the upper-band exit is much rarer and would spend most of its time in choppy drawdown waiting for the full bounce.

When it works

In range-bound markets and on assets that have a strong underlying support level. The lower-band breach is rare in calm markets and reliably tags genuine short-term oversold conditions; the centerline exit captures the easier half of the reversion. This strategy and RSI mean reversion both fade dips, but they read "oversold" differently and tend to disagree at the edges where one signal triggers and the other does not.

When it fails

In sustained downtrends. The lower band itself drifts down with the SMA, so a strategy that bought at the lower band can find the band move below it before the bounce materializes. Worse: the volatility spike during a breakdown widens the band, so the entry threshold moves further from the SMA at exactly the moment the strategy most needs a tight band. The 2008 financial crisis and the March 2020 collapse both punished long-only mean-reversion strategies broadly, including Bollinger.

Parameters

  • period: rolling-window length, in bars. Bollinger's original 1983 recommendation is 20. Range: 2-200.
  • k: number of standard deviations from the SMA to draw the bands. Bollinger's original recommendation is 2.0; this captures ~95% of bars if returns were normally distributed (they aren't, but it's a reasonable starting point). Range: 0.5-4.0.

The Rust

The implementation walks the bar series once. For each bar, slices the rolling window of length period ending at the current bar, computes the mean and the population standard deviation, derives the lower band, and updates the position state. The population std (divide by N) matches the convention every charting platform uses; the sample std (divide by N - 1) would be marginally smaller and shift the band threshold slightly. The Wikipedia and Bollinger's own book definition use population, so we match.
