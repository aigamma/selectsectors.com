SMA crossover.

Long when the fast simple moving average is above the slow simple moving average; flat otherwise. The textbook trend-following signal and one of the easiest to reason about.

Intuition

The fast SMA tracks recent price action; the slow SMA tracks longer-term price action. When the fast crosses above the slow, recent prices are trending higher than the long-run baseline, which we interpret as a confirmed uptrend and go long. When the fast falls back below the slow, the uptrend is broken and we exit to flat.

The crossover lags the actual trend reversal because moving averages are inherently lagged. The trade-off: smaller windows catch reversals sooner but produce more false-positive whipsaws; larger windows are smoother but enter and exit later.

When it works

In persistent uptrends (think tech bull runs 2017-2021 or 2023-2024 on the top-by-volume single names). The strategy stays long through the trend and captures most of the move. Sharpe in these regimes can comfortably exceed buy and hold's because the max drawdown is reduced; the strategy exits to flat during corrections rather than riding them down.

When it fails

In choppy sideways markets. The fast SMA crosses above and below the slow repeatedly, generating whipsaw entries and exits that each cost a small loss in the round-trip. Death by a thousand cuts. The historical regime where SMA crossover underperformed buy and hold worst was 2015-2016, where the index was approximately flat and the crossover thrashed.

Parameters

  • fast: length of the fast SMA window, in bars. Typical values: 5-50. Defaults to 20.
  • slow: length of the slow SMA window, in bars. Typical values: 50-200. Defaults to 50. Must be strictly greater than fast.

The Rust crate validates fast < slow and bars.len() >= slow up front; bad params return a clean InvalidParam or NotEnoughBars error before any computation runs.
