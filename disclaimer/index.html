What the backtests show

A backtest on this site is a hypothesis test against historical data. It takes a strategy specification (the entry and exit rules, the parameters), applies it to past daily bar data for one symbol over a date range, and reports the metrics that fall out (total return, CAGR, Sharpe, drawdown, hit rate). The result is mathematically honest: the no-lookahead engine enforces that signals at bar i only act on bar i+1 onwards. It is not predictive of any future trading result.

Reasons a backtest result will not match live trading performance, in roughly decreasing order of impact:

  1. Slippage and commissions. Bar-close fills in a backtest are an idealization. Real fills cross the spread, eat commissions, and on illiquid names move the market against you. See /philosophy/backtest-vs-live/ for the long form.
  2. Survivorship bias. The eleven anchor single names this site exposes are the 2026 top-by- options-volume list. Backtesting them against pre-2026 data overstates the result because the names that fell out of the ranking are not in the universe. See /philosophy/survivorship-bias/.
  3. Overfitting. A strategy whose Sharpe looks great at one specific parameter setting on one specific historical window is almost always worse out- of-sample. See /philosophy/overfitting/.
  4. Regime dependence. Trend-following strategies look great in trending markets and ugly in chop. Mean-reversion strategies do the opposite. A backtest covers one regime; the next regime may be different.
  5. Tax treatment. Backtests report pre-tax returns. A high-turnover strategy pays the higher short-term tax rate; the after-tax Sharpe is often substantially worse than the pre-tax Sharpe.

What the chatbot is and isn't

SelectBot is a focused educational chatbot scoped to four topics: Rust (especially as this site uses it), the site itself, quant finance basics, and the philosophy of backtesting. It refuses to give investment advice or predict stock prices. If you try to use it as a stock- picking oracle it will redirect you. Treat its answers as starting points for further reading, not as authoritative conclusions. It is a language model with the limitations all language models have: occasional confident wrongness, knowledge that lags real-time events, no awareness of your specific financial situation.

Data freshness and accuracy

The bar data this site queries lives in a Supabase project shared with aigamma.com and is updated weekdays after the US market close. There can be gaps for symbols whose history is shallower than the date range you pick (the eleven anchor single names start 2024-04-25 in the shared database; SPX goes back to 2022-01-03; the SPDR sectors are in between). Bar accuracy is whatever the upstream Massive Stocks Starter and Indices Starter feeds provide; we don't add corrections beyond what the feed delivers.

Code quality and reliability

The Rust strategy crate has 39 unit tests covering numerical correctness and parameter validation. The TypeScript helpers (canonicalize, sha256, rate-limit) have 21 unit tests. The CI workflow runs all of them on every push. None of this guarantees the math is correct in every edge case; cite the source if a result looks suspicious (the source is published at github.com/aigamma/selectsectors.com and the curriculum pages inline it via build-time imports so what you see is what runs).
