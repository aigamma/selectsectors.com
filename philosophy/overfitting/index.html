Overfitting is the default.

The single biggest hazard in backtesting. If you tune a strategy's parameters to maximize Sharpe on a historical window, the live result will almost certainly be worse than the backtest suggested. Two defenses actually work; most others do not.

What the failure looks like

You design a strategy. You give it three parameters. You run a grid search across plausible values for each. Hundreds of combinations later, you find one that produces a Sharpe of 3.2 over the last five years. You ship it. Six months later it's down 8%.

This is not bad luck. It is the default outcome. With enough parameter combinations on any fixed historical window, you will find a combination that "works" by chance. The selected combination is the one whose specific noise aligned with the specific past most favorably, and that alignment is not predictive of the future.

Why this happens, mathematically

Each parameter setting is a test against the same data. If you run N tests, the probability that the best one looks good by chance scales with N. The classic example: flip 1000 coins, the best one came up heads 56 times out of 100. That looks like skill but it's just the maximum order statistic of the binomial distribution. Strategy parameter sweeps are the same thing with extra dimensions.

The cleaner you tune to a window, the worse the out-of-sample result tends to be. This is the bias-variance trade-off applied to strategy design.

Defense 1: out-of-sample holdout

Split your data into two windows. Tune your strategy on the first window (the in-sample data). Evaluate it on the second window (the out-of-sample data) without further tuning. Report the out-of-sample result, not the in-sample one. If the strategy degrades materially out-of-sample, it was overfit.

The honest version of this is to make the out-of-sample split before you start tuning, write it down somewhere inaccessible during tuning, and report the result whether it confirms your hypothesis or not. The dishonest version (keep "re-evaluating" on the out-of-sample data, picking the version that worked best there too) is overfitting to the out-of-sample data and gives you no real signal.

Defense 2: parameter-neighborhood stability

The other defense is to look at the strategy's Sharpe across a neighborhood of parameter settings, not just the single best one. If the best SMA(20, 50) has Sharpe 1.5 and SMA(15, 50), SMA(25, 50), SMA(20, 40), SMA(20, 60) all have Sharpes in 1.2-1.7, the strategy is robust. If those neighbors have Sharpes of -0.3, 0.4, 0.1, 0.6, you found a Sharpe-1.5 spike in noise.

The most honest single-number summary of a strategy is something like "median Sharpe across reasonable parameter variations on out-of-sample data" rather than "best Sharpe on in-sample data." The first one will usually be lower. That's the price of honesty.
