Regimes.

A strategy that worked great across the 2024 bull market can lose money across 2025 if the regime shifts. Backtests summarize behavior across whatever regimes happened to be in the test window. Reading the result honestly means knowing which regimes the window covered, which one is live now, and how the strategy is likely to behave in each.

What is a regime?

A regime is a stretch of market behavior with stable statistical character. The character can be defined many ways; the three most useful axes are:

  • Direction. Trending up, trending down, or sideways. Defined operationally as the sign of the slope of a long-window (250-bar) moving average.
  • Volatility. Calm (annualized realized vol under 12%) or stressed (over 25%). Defined operationally as the rolling 30-day std dev of daily returns, times sqrt(252).
  • Mean reversion vs trend persistence. The autocorrelation of daily returns. Positive autocorrelation = trends self-perpetuate. Negative autocorrelation = bounces follow drops. Zero = random walk.

Combining these three gives a regime grid: trending + calm, trending + stressed, sideways + calm, sideways + stressed, etc. Different strategies prefer different cells.

Strategy regime preferences

The strategies in this site's library all have regime preferences worth naming explicitly:

  • SMA crossover / momentum / breakout. Trend-following. Want trending + calm. The trend provides the directional bet; calm volatility means fewer whipsaw signals.
  • RSI mean reversion / Bollinger Bands. Mean-reverting. Want sideways + range-bound. The sideways behavior is the source of the edge; calm volatility keeps the bands tight enough that breaches are meaningful.
  • Buy and hold. Wants trending up. Is agnostic to volatility (within reason; high-vol trending-up markets still favor it but the drawdowns during corrections are larger).

A backtest that ran a momentum strategy through a 2022 equity-bear, 2023 chop, and 2024 bull market will show a decent overall Sharpe but the three sub-periods would look wildly different. Most of the return is the 2024 tail; the 2022 and 2023 sub-periods would show flat-to- negative returns. The Sharpe averages over the regimes; the realized P&L depends on which regime you happen to deploy into.

How to detect the live regime

The honest answer: you cannot, with high confidence, in real time. Regime labels are easy to assign in retrospect; the boundary between "noisy chop" and "the start of a new trend" is exactly the kind of signal that fools every momentum-detection algorithm in the literature.

What you can do: look at the current rolling vol vs its long-run distribution, look at the sign of the long-window moving average, and look at the recent realized autocorrelation. Together these give you a probabilistic read on which regime you're in. If your strategy is trend-following and the rolling vol is unusually high while autocorrelation is negative, you're probably about to get chopped. That's not certainty, but it's better than ignoring the regime entirely.

What to do about it

Three honest options, in increasing order of complexity:

  1. Accept the regime risk. Buy and hold is the simplest example: you accept that drawdowns will be deep during bear markets and that you'll just sit through them. The historical equity premium has paid buy-and-holders to bear this risk; the future may or may not.
  2. Match strategy to regime in real time. Trade trend-following strategies when the regime is trending, switch to mean reversion when it's sideways. Operationally: run a regime classifier, gate strategy execution on its output. The hard part is the classifier; in practice these tend to lag the regime change by enough that the gating eats most of the benefit.
  3. Combine strategies that are uncorrelated across regimes. Run a trend-following strategy and a mean-reversion strategy together with sized positions. Their returns partially cancel during choppy markets (the mean-reversion makes money while the trend-follower loses) and partially compound during trending markets (the trend-follower makes money while the mean-reversion loses). The combined Sharpe is often higher than either standalone, and the max drawdown is meaningfully smaller. This is the simplest version of "factor diversification".
The honest takeaway. A single backtest number on a single regime-mix is one data point. Run the same strategy on multiple non-overlapping sub-periods (call them folds); look at the Sharpe distribution across folds; that variation is your regime-uncertainty estimate. If the per-fold Sharpe ranges from -1 to +3, the strategy is high-variance under regime change; if it ranges from +0.5 to +1.5, it's more robust. The single-window backtest hides this entirely.
