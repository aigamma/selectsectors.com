The philosophy of backtesting.

A backtest is a hypothesis test against historical data, not a prediction. These essays cover the four most common ways backtests lie and how to tell whether the strategy you're looking at is the start of something real or the best of a thousand overfit candidates.

Essays

  1. 01 Overfitting is the default If you tune a strategy's params to maximize Sharpe on a historical window, the out-of-sample result is almost certainly worse than the backtest suggests. Two defenses that actually work.
  2. 02 Survivorship bias Backtesting on today's universe ignores the names that rotated out. NVDA today, GME or AMC five years ago. The universe selection is as important as the strategy.
  3. 03 Lookahead bias Using a value in your signal that wouldn't have been known at the time. The most common version is using close[i] to set position[i]. How this site's engine enforces the no-lookahead guarantee structurally.
  4. 04 Backtest vs live trading Even a perfect backtest is an upper bound on live performance. Slippage, commissions, partial fills, market hours, taxes, and your own size moving the market on illiquid names.
  5. 05 Regimes Trending vs mean-reverting, high-vol vs low-vol, crisis vs normal. Why a strategy that worked across one regime can fail in the next, and what to do about it.

The right question

"What Sharpe does this strategy show in a backtest?" is the wrong question. The right question is: "what is the distribution of Sharpe across reasonable parameter settings on out-of-sample data, and how does it compare to buy and hold on the same universe?" If a strategy beats buy and hold meaningfully across reasonable variations on out-of- sample data, it might be real. If it only beats buy and hold at one specific tuning on in-sample data, it almost certainly isn't.
