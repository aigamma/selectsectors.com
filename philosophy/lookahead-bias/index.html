Lookahead bias.

Using a value in your signal calculation that wouldn't have been known at the time you needed it. The bug is easy to write, hard to spot, and produces backtests that look spectacular until you go live.

The canonical version

You write a backtest that does: 

for i in 0..bars.len() {
  let signal = compute_signal(&bars[..=i]);
  let return_for_today = (bars[i].close - bars[i-1].close) / bars[i-1].close;
  pnl[i] = signal * return_for_today;
}

Notice the bug: the signal at bar i is computed using bars up to and including i's close, but the return at bar i is the price change from i-1 to i. The signal is using close[i], which is the same close that generated the return you're trying to predict. In live trading you would only learn close[i] at the end of bar i; you cannot have already taken the position at the start of bar i based on close[i].

A backtest with this bug shows wildly inflated Sharpe because the signal is correlated with the realized return by construction. The strategy "predicts" a bar that is part of its input.

How this site solves it

The fix is to lag the position by one bar: the position at the close of bar i determines the exposure during bar i+1. The Rust engine enforces this in apply_positions_to_bars: 

for i in 1..bars.len() {
  let bar_return = (bars[i].close - bars[i-1].close) / bars[i-1].close;
  let exposure = positions[i - 1];  // <-- lagged one bar
  returns.push(exposure * bar_return);
}

The lag is the centralized place where the no-lookahead guarantee lives. Strategies in this crate produce a position vector; they never compute realized returns themselves. So even if a strategy author accidentally used close[i] to set positions[i] (which is legal because the close is realized at the end of bar i and we're setting the position for the start of bar i+1), the math comes out right.

The subtler version

Backtesting frameworks that compute "yesterday's signal, today's fill at yesterday's close" are usually safe. Frameworks that compute "signal at bar i, fill at bar i's open or close" are usually buggy. The line between "the bar's close is realized at the end of the bar" and "the close is the only price you can use as your fill" is subtle; a well-designed engine forces the right answer by construction, as ours does.

Other places lookahead bias hides:

  • Restated economic data. If your signal uses GDP or earnings data, are you using the value as originally reported or the post-restatement value? Real time only has the original report.
  • Survivor-adjusted prices. Some price series back-adjust for splits, dividends, or spin-offs. If the adjustment uses information that wouldn't have been known at the time, the series itself has lookahead.
  • Universe membership. Backtesting "the S&P 500 as of today" includes companies that weren't in the index five years ago. The point-in-time universe is what a real trader would have held.
The structural rule. Build your backtest so that the only way to make it produce a result is to use information that would have been available at the time. Make it easy to do the right thing and impossible to do the wrong thing. This site's apply_positions_to_bars is one example of that principle.
