Backtest vs live trading.

A backtest assumes you got the prices in the bars at the prices in the bars. Real fills include slippage, commissions, partial fills, market hours, taxes, and the fact that your size moves the market on illiquid names. Even a perfect backtest is an upper bound on what live trading will produce.

The six things a backtest gets wrong

1. Slippage

The "fill price" in a backtest is usually the close of the bar you decided to trade on. In live trading the actual fill is some distance from the close: a market order crosses the spread (immediate cost), a limit order may not fill at all (opportunity cost). On the universe this site backtests, slippage is small but not zero; on less liquid names it can swamp the strategy's edge entirely.

2. Commissions

Most retail brokers in 2026 charge $0 per equity trade, but options commissions, market-data fees, and the regulatory fees baked into every transaction still apply. For a strategy with thousands of trades per year these add up to meaningful drag.

3. Partial fills and order book friction

A backtest assumes you executed your full intended size. Live trading on names with thin liquidity often fills partially or at progressively worse prices as the order works through the book. The 100-share retail trader rarely notices; the 10,000-share institutional trader plans around it.

4. Market hours and gaps

Daily-bar backtests assume you can execute at the close. What if the strategy fires a signal at Friday's close and the market gaps 5% over the weekend? The backtest sees the gap as a free move; live trading sees it as a forced choice between holding the gap risk and exiting at whatever Monday's open offers.

5. Taxes

A backtest reports pre-tax returns. A strategy that generates many short-term gains pays the higher short-term tax rate; the same strategy holding longer gets the long-term rate. The after-tax Sharpe of a high-turnover strategy can be substantially worse than its before-tax Sharpe.

6. Your size moves the market

The biggest backtest-vs-live gap for institutional strategies, and the least relevant for retail. If your strategy says "buy 10% of XLE's daily volume", you cannot actually buy that without moving the price against yourself. Backtests usually ignore market impact; the strategies that look best in backtest are often the ones most vulnerable to it.

What honest backtest reporting looks like

Cite the strategy's Sharpe alongside its turnover (trades per year) and an estimate of slippage per trade. A 1.5 Sharpe at 50 trades per year is more robust than a 2.5 Sharpe at 500 trades per year because the latter is closer to the slippage-eats-the-edge regime. Where possible, back-test against realistic fill assumptions: don't use close-to-close; use close-to-next-open or close-plus-half- spread.

This site's backtests use close-to-close because the strategies are daily-bar and the universe is liquid; the slippage assumption is implicit and small but real. Production deployment of any of these strategies should add an explicit slippage model and re-evaluate the Sharpe under it.

The mental adjustment. Discount any backtest's Sharpe by 30-50% in your head as a default before assuming the strategy is live-tradable. The actual discount is strategy-specific, but the bias is always in one direction and is always meaningful.
